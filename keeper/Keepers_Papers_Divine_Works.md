# The Keeper's Papers — The Divine Work Engine

*Companion to Book III, §3.7. Keeper-facing. Not printed in the Crew's Packet.*
*Status: drafted from the v3 review. Ratified items are marked ✓; open items are marked ◻.*

The player Handbook says only what a player needs: a Divine Work is weather the keeper
sets moving, it ends on a turning, and it throws off **Footholds** the crew can grasp.
Everything below is the machinery under that — how you build one and run it.

---

## 1. The three tiers ✓ (guidance, not hard rules)

Same engine at every size; only the span and the patience differ.

- **Episode** — one sitting's worth; a single strong beat.
- **Arc** — several sittings; a handful of chained beats.
- **Saga** — one or more seasons; beats that may sleep a long time between arming.

Tiers are a pacing aid for you, never a constraint the fiction has to obey.

---

## 2. Beats ✓

A Divine Work is a chain of **beats**. A beat does nothing until its **Trigger** is met.
When the trigger fires, the beat's **Clock** starts, and only then does time bite.

A beat is written in three lines:

- **Trigger** — the fictional condition that arms it.
- **Clock** — how many watches once armed (may be **0** — fires the instant it arms).
- **Break** — what the beat does when the clock runs out: usually a signed squall
  (letterhead, never omniscient narration), sometimes a rewrite of the card itself
  (recast the aim, add a stake, release another beat).

Beats may **branch**: one beat's Break can open several possible *next* beats, chosen by
what you know at the moment it breaks. (Example: a *first-contact* beat with Clock 0 arms
the moment the crew reaches a place, then opens whichever follow-on beats the meeting warrants.)

---

## 3. "Non-step-able" ✓ — the two guarantees

1. An **un-armed** beat cannot be advanced, forced, or grace'd forward. No player lever
   moves a clock that has not started. (This is what "The storm shouldn't tick until the
   enemy is ready to be an enemy" means in rules.)
2. Once **armed**, the clock is **yours** — it runs on the terms you set at declaring, not
   on the tide's default one-per-session cadence.

---

## 4. What arms a trigger ✓ (mix freely)

- A **story condition** you watch for (a faction reaches striking distance).
- A **completed or failed Work** (a well is poisoned; a shield is raised).
- A **hidden date** on the countdown you may quietly keep (§3.7: "even if one is quietly kept").
- A **player action** that pokes the sleeping thing.

---

## 5. The Weatherglass ✓ — advance notice, the squall's twin

By default the clock is **yours and hidden**: the crew feels the squeeze without seeing the
number. But the fiction may hand them a **weatherglass** — a way to read a coming turn early:
long-range sensors, an intercepted order, a defector's word, a prophecy half-heard. A
weatherglass makes a specific armed clock **visible** before it breaks, turning dread into a
race they can run.

Rule of thumb: the squall is bad news that *arrives*; the weatherglass is bad news the crew
*sees coming*. Both are earned through the fiction, never simply granted.

◻ **Open:** default visibility. Ruling this chapter: **keeper's own and hidden by default,
made visible only by an earned weatherglass.** Confirm this is the standing default before print.

---

## 6. Footholds ✓ — the graspable Works

A Divine Work cannot be seized head-on. It throws off ordinary **Works** the crew *can* grasp —
its **Footholds**. Each Foothold is a normal card (Aim / Hands / Stakes), marked with a small
cursive **ℓ** in the corner to show the greater tide it serves.

**Recommended habit (not a hard law):** every *armed* beat should expose at least one Foothold —
a Work the crew can actually pour Hands into to change how the beat lands. A beat with no
Foothold is something that happens *to* the players; that is railroading by another name.
Give them a handhold and let their choices bend the weather.

---

## 7. The limit on the maker's hand ✓

From §2.2 (Grace) and carried here: **divine intervention goes only as far as a faction's
nature.** Grace and Standing can nudge a people, open a door, soften a blow — but they cannot
make a people other than what they are. When you decide whether a faction will lift a hand,
ask first what that people *is*; the answer is the ceiling.

---

## 8. Worked shape (generic)

> **Divine Work — "The Siege"** (Arc)
>
> - **Beat I · Gathering** — *Trigger:* the enemy gains a foothold within reach. *Clock:* none
>   (mood only). *Break:* rumors, refugees, a first signed warning.
> - **Beat II · The Glass** — *Trigger:* the crew builds or wins a lookout (a Foothold ℓ).
>   *Clock:* 0. *Break:* a **weatherglass** — the crew now sees Beat III's clock.
> - **Beat III · Landfall** — *Trigger:* the enemy commits. *Clock:* 3 watches.
>   *Break:* the outer defense fails, signed by the officer who held it. Exposes two Footholds ℓ
>   (hold the line / evacuate).
> - **Beat IV · The Turning** — *Trigger:* Beat III resolves, either way. *Break:* relief arrives,
>   or the price is paid; the Work ends on this turning, not on a watch. One law still holds: **mercy**.

Swap the fiction; the skeleton is the engine.

---

## 9. Cross-references

- **Reversion** (§4.3–4.4): a Foothold left unheld slips back a watch each watch, like any Work.
  Pulling a head onto a Divine-Work emergency has real cost.
- **A favor owed** (§3.5): a sealed price named now, held unread by the crew, opened only when
  called — a good instrument for a Break that comes due much later.
- **Testimony is king** (§3.7): every beat, every squall, arrives on someone's letterhead.
